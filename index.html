The Gadfly Project

  • What: Gadfly Project Web
  • When: 2016
  • Where: Degree Capstone Project
  • I did: User Research, Product Vision, Conversational Design & Slack Bot Development, User Testing

What is The Gadfly Project?

The Gadfly Project is an automatic question generation service. If you input text, it outputs questions. This was the result of questions we have attempted to answer over the last several months in cycles of research and design.
Here's a handy poster I designed to explain: Our initial hypothesis was that our service could be used as comment-CAPTCHA. If you want to comment on an article, you will be asked a question generated by our service to prove that you actually read the article. So, we interviewed three local newspaper publishers (Berkeleyside, SFGate, Daily Cal) and the CEO of Disqus, a comment moderation provider, to better understand the online commenting space.

We used a survey to understand people's news reading and online commenting behavior. Within 10 days, we received 181 responses with an 82% completion rate! The survey’s most important finding was that 77% of the respondents preferred to answer a question instead of creating an account to leave a comment. We decided to conduct interviews with some of these respondents to learn more.

Apart from contributing to the user research, I defined the product vision for us by writing down what I imagined The Gadfly Project could be. This helped our team communicate in more concrete terms.
You can read it here

At this point in the project, we needed a way to quickly get feedback from people about the questions being generated by the API. The I School Slack provided us easy access to several users and allowed us to quickly gather feedback and iterate. I designed and developed 2 different Slack Bots -- QBot (uses our API to quiz users on the news) and TriviaBot (conducts small trivia games using our API). They essentially interfaces that allow users to converse with our API in a controlled manner. We used them for testing the usability of our API. Below you can see an example interaction a user can have with QBot.

This is a screenshot of an actual trivia session we organized during our live demo!
QBot and TriviaBot are good examples of what a curious developer can build using our API. If you have an ischool.berkeley.edu email address, you can join ischool.slack.com and try out our bots there!

Our focus in the future is improving the API and building out a platform that can enable other developers to explore the potential of question generation.


Yackle

  • What: Q&A Community for the UC Berkeley I School
  • When: 2016
  • Where:
  • I did: User Research, User Experience Design, Front-End Development

Problem: There are no trustworthy platforms for members of academic communities to share their knowledge within their communities and outside the community.

Our Value Proposition: Helping communities share and grow their knowledge.

We want to create a primary medium to ask questions of a private academic community such as the I School and get answers you can trust.

So, we built a private Q&A tool where people can ask questions to trusted communities, privately or publicly. Right now, we have 106 users (including incoming students, current students, alumni and admin staff) engaging with the website.

The sign up page that uses LinkedIn OAuth. After usability testing, we added copy to inform users why we require them to log in using LinkedIn and what we do with their data.

The main view is different questions asked by community members. The main UI elements are highlighted.

When a user clicks on a question, they see the individual question and answers. The interface elements maintain familiarity with the previous page.

The user can add their own answer here. We added a small line (the "no edit hack" above) to promote more mindfulness while answering.

If a user clicks on the ask button on the home page they see this screen. We kept this page very simple to avoid distractions.

I carried out user research (interviews, usability testing, card sort) to identify short term fixes and long term improvements.

Insights from the research have helped us prioritize the items on our roadmap. Eg. We want to Improve Discovery [by providing Search or other Discovery mechanism], Improve Engagement [using Notifications, Feeds], allow users to target questions at subject matter experts. For the long term product strategy, we want to drive wider adoption [through a Slack integration] and scale the system to handle several communities.

TweetDay

  • What: Visualizing your time away from Twitter
  • When: Jan 2015
  • I did: Research, Interaction Design, Front-end Development

In Case You Missed It. People and organizations use the acronym ICYMI to bring content to the attention of those who may have missed it before. I have been a Twitter user for 7 years now and really love using it. So, I would be perplexed when I met people who said "Oh I don't really know how to use Twitter" or "There's just so much happening on Twitter that I can't keep up."
I wanted to create something that would show me what I missed when I went away from Twitter.
One of our testers remarked, "This is actually making me want to log in to my twitter account more!"

Try out the app here: tweetday.co

I worked on the TreeMap-style grid for displaying tweets. Users can choose to sort either by retweets or favorites and filter based on the kind of multimedia. More screenshots below:

Approach:

In order to verify our hypothesis, we interviewed a diverse range of people and found that even self-identified "savvy" respondents were frustrated with the amount of information that they had to deal with.
 After we validated the key pain points, our research turned to academic papers for inspiration. We based our designs on two academic visualizations we learned about in class -- Seesoft and TwitArcs. We found financial websites using TreeMaps to display a broad range of varied information daily. These combined helped us to display a large amount of tweets in a limited space. It also allowed the user to take control of their timeline and filter based on retweets, favorites, or media type (picture/video).

Using TwitArcs we show replies between different users. By following these arcs, users can trace connections between tweets and identify common themes if any. You can see me talking to Ryan Hoover (founder of Product Hunt) about Groundhog Day below!

Using Seesoft we could present a vaste amount of text (about 2000 tweet worth!) in a single area on the left. Each thin row represented a tweet and on hover the text of the tweet is displayed in a modal panel. In the screenshot below, I dragged the ends of the scale to see only tweets from a certain period.

You can filter to see just images or videos if you wish.

We had great reactions from our users:
"This is definitely something that I would want to use."
"I don’t think anything like this exists and I would like to use it when it comes out."

Overall, I felt like we had delivered on what we set out to make. But I don't think we solved all that we thought we would solve. Some of the issues that we can fix are introducing an onboarding process so that users can familiarize themselves with the different elements. Also, as the contact area is so small, users have to hover very precisely over each element in the block to see the individual tweets. Twitter has no trouble retaining me, a power user, but it can be confusing and it can even be repellent for new users. This does not quite solve that problem. It does provide a different way for existing users (who are struggling with the information overload) to keep in touch. However, I definitely think the TweetDay experience can be bettered as we work on known issues and continue to iterate on the designs going forward.

Clover

  • What: A collaborative "endless walking" game
  • When: Fall 2015
  • Where: Critical Practices
  • I did: Participant Observation, Laser Cutting, 3D Printing, A LOT of Gluing Things Together

Clover is a community oriented video game that aims to reduce social anxiety while waiting, and promote cooperative social interactions between members.

. We had to build something for a community and the one we chose was the dinner line that forms in Cloyne Court, the student housing cooperative—the biggest in the US—located at on the north side of the UC Berkeley campus. Here's a video of the game being played by some members of Cloyne Court.

One of my team mates used to live in Cloyne so we knew that students had issues with the dinner line. We did participant observations at dinner time for three days and also interviewed residents while they waited to find out more.
The key findings from the interview were that members often had to get in line early because if they don't they might not get any food at all. Quite a few students mentioned that they feel the line has become uncooperative over the semesters.

One of our team mates was really good at game design so we were happy to leverage here expertise and make a collaborative game. Here are some of the ideas we sketched during our brainstorming. We borrowed bowls from the Cloyne dinner hall and taped our laser-cut buttons on the top. Each bowl contained a raspberry pi to transmit user input (pressing the button) over WiFi to our Node.js server. The player has to press a button to make the chameleon change its color to the color of the leaf coming up. The challenge is that there are just 3 buttons: red, blue and yellow. So, player has to coordinate with other players to match the secondary colors. For example, players need to press red and blue to match a green leaf. The resulting interaction is not just cooperative, but challenging and funny as you can be seen in this video where some of the members of the community are playing it:



Players loved the high score feature, enjoyed the bowl integration, and — get this — were even looking forward to the dinner line the next day. We also found out that there was delay due to the WiFi latency and unclear affordances. It took players a while to figure out that they need to match the color of the next, not current, leaf.

In the future in terms of design, we want to revisit controller design & form factors. The bowls were cumbersome and we found it hard to debug the hardware components inside. We also want to explore different feedback methods besides color change (such as music, walking speed).

You can try out the game (minus the cool buttons) at queuemeleon.herokuapp.com on a desktop browser. Click the title screen to begin the game.

Pronto

  • What: Android App
  • When: Oct 2014
  • Where: Play Conference
  • I did: Concept Generation, Low Fidelity Prototyping, Android App Design & Development
  • Awards: Best Overall Prize and the Audience Prize

Problem Statement: Small businesses are the backbone of U.S. economy – over 50% of the population works for SMB and 500k SMBs are started each month, and the statistics are similar in other countries. Yet, SMBs face unique challenges on their path to success, on how to succeed in a new market, or compete against much larger incumbents. Hack together a working prototype of an application or mobile application that will help small business owners.
Value Proposition: Helping local small businesses manage their excess inventory by connecting them with customers.

We visited local stores (cupcake stores!), watched customers at the stores, and interviewed store owners. We wanted to understand their life, environment, behaviors, what problems do they have and what hacks they use to solve these problems.


A common complaint we heard was that business owners often had wasted excess inventory at the end of the day or they would have downtime with no customers. We wanted to help SMBs such as food shops and hair salons. We found enough insights and evidence to validate our idea. So what do we think is the solution?

We started off with an auction model. However, after talking about this idea with our mentor and seeing him struggle to comprehend the business model, it dawned on us that this would be too complicated for a SMB to quickly grasp and use. This prompted our team to change our design to be a much more simplified purchasing model for each timeslot.

Create a channel to get rid of the excess inventory as well as to get new customers into their store.
Connect local SMBs and customers via this channel.
Make the channel less intrusive and simple.



We designed Pronto such that users who downloaded the app would only receive one offer, called a “Pronto”, every hour, on the hour for a place within 20 minutes walking distance. Once they received the offer, they had only 60 minutes to redeem the offer. We wanted to build both anticipation as well as the fear of missing out for each offer.

For the Business app, users would see a timeslot that has expired, prompting a reactionary desire to purchase the next timeslot. The next timeslot shows a time to expiration and remaining coupons (or 'prontos'). The business owners can only purchase for the next two hours, which focuses them on an immediate purchase, and reduces additional contemplation or analysis. After he/she has chosen a timeslot, the screen then prompts them to adjust how many 'prontos' to send out to consumers with the app. The price of these deals is displayed as a round number to reduce complexity.

Learned Android in 4 hours!
Developed the Business end of the app.
Developed the mapping features on the Customer end of the app.
Won the Best Overall Prize and the Audience Prize.

Yapi Kapi

  • What: Web Application
  • When: Sep 2014
  • Where: Hack the Hearst Hackathon
  • I did: Brainstorming, Feature Design & Development
  • Award: Best Overall App and Best Heritage App

Problem Statement: To develop a compelling application and/or user interface for the digital collections data that the Hearst Museum is making available through an API.

"..address issues of scholarship, and show applicability and adaptability to a range of projects and scholarly fields."
"Children won't interact with just a website. They like to play games."
"I came to the museum many times to see the objects made by my people and my family. I want other people interested in our culture to be able to see a more complete description."

We created a tool for K–12 students to view all cataloged artifacts from a region by drawing a geographic shape onto a map. We layered the information discovery process so that there is no overload. In the end a historical "story" [yapi kapi means 'your story' in Lakota] about the people being uncovered should be seen.

The goal is to bring the artifacts to life for young children and help users explore the Hearst Museum's collection freely and to develop their own path of interests. We envisioned a large map as the entrypoint of the design with an interactive search feature. The search feature, built with Javascript & Google Maps, asks users to draw a shape around areas they would like to explore, and Yapi Kapi returns a list of results from that area.

We also developed teacher-student tools in Yapi Kapi, such as the ability to let teachers assign prompts and homework. Teachers are also able to create accounts for their classes, with sub-accounts for each student. Students can then explore the museum collection through Yapi Kapi, and add any interesting artifacts to their "satchel."

We wanted students to be able to explore freely, and to later reflect on what they found interesting.
